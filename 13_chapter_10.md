# **10.6 What Constraints Shape a Script?  
(The Real Conditions Faced by an Indus Scribe)**

Every script in human history is shaped not only by imagination  
but by **constraints**‚Äîmaterial, cultural, cognitive, and functional.

An Indus scribe worked under conditions such as:

### **Material constraints**
- steatite is soft when carved but hard after firing  
- seals are small (often 2‚Äì3 cm)  
- lines must be shallow, consistent, and durable  
- mistakes are hard to correct  
- clay impressions must remain readable

### **Cognitive constraints**
- symbols must be memorable  
- shapes must be recognisable at a glance  
- variation must not confuse users

### **Cultural constraints**
- symbols must resonate with shared experiences  
  (animals, tools, rituals, clan identities)

### **Functional constraints**
- signs must fit into the prefix‚Äìcore‚Äìsuffix structure  
- sequences must be short  
- seals must work like ‚Äúidentity + category‚Äù markers

These constraints shaped the evolution  
of Indus symbols just as much as creativity did.

# **10.7 Why Abstraction Was Necessary (Not Optional)**

Indus seals are tiny.

# **10.6 Why Some Designs Survive and Others Disappear**

When a community accepts a sign, it must satisfy three conditions:

### **1. Practicality**  
The sign must be easy to carve repeatedly on:

- steatite  
- copper tablets  
- pottery  
- seals and sealings  

If it takes too long or requires too much precision,  
it will not survive across generations.

### **2. Recognisability**  
A good symbol is one that even a child or an untrained adult  
can identify instantly.

This is why abstract forms evolve:

- horns become V-shapes  
- jars become circles with a stem  
- fish become lines with a fork  
- humans become stick figures  
- gateways become rectangles  

### **3. Cultural Agreement**  
A society must **accept** a sign  
before it enters the official symbol system.

Thousands of seals across Indus sites  
show the *same* core forms.  
This implies a long process of testing, teaching, revising,  
and standardising ‚Äî exactly as you experienced  
when designing the ACTC logo.

Only the **best** designs endure.
