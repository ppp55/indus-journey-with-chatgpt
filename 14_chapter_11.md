# **Chapter 11 ‚Äî The Story of Early Numbers:  
From Clay Marks to Indus Strokes & Tamil Digits (Part 1)**

Before numerals became symbols, numbers lived in **marks**.

Early humans expressed quantity through:

- strokes,  
- cuts,

- tallies,  
- repeated lines,  
- grouped objects.

This chapter traces how these simple marks evolved into:

- Indus stroke modifiers,  
- Sumerian wedge-counts,  
- early Tamil tally-marks,

It helps students understand that  
**mathematical thinking begins long before mathematical notation.**

## **11.1 The Oldest Numbers Were Not ‚ÄúNumbers‚Äù**

- Sumerian proto-writing,

Numbers begin as **repetition**.

## **11.4 Indus Strokes: The Missing Link in Early Number Logic**

Indus inscriptions frequently contain:

- 1 stroke  
- 2 strokes  
- 3 strokes  
- 4 strokes  

These are placed:

- as **prefixes**,  
- as **suffixes**,  
- next to **core signs**,  
- sometimes by themselves.

Even without deciphering meaning,  
students can see that:

### **Strokes modify meaning.**  
They ‚Äúadd something‚Äù to the core sign.

### **Strokes are controlled.**  
We almost never see more than 4.

### **Strokes follow position rules.**  
Usually left or right of the main symbol.

### **Strokes create families.**  
A sign + 1 stroke,  
A sign + 2 strokes,  
A sign + 3 strokes  
form a clear structural series.

This behaviour matches early counting traditions globally.

## **11.6 Why Early Humans Used Strokes for Numbers**

Before numerals existed, the human mind relied on **visible repetition**.

Across cultures:

- a single mark = 1  
- two marks = 2  
- three marks = 3  
- four marks = 4 (sometimes crossed as 5)

This appears in:

- Upper Paleolithic bones  
- Sumerian tallies  
- Egyptian early numerals  
- Chinese oracle bone signs  
- early Tamil marks  
- Indus stroke signs

Why strokes?

Because strokes are:

- quick to draw  
- easy to repeat  
- impossible to confuse  
- universally understood  
- independent of language

This is why children even today count with sticks, pebbles, or lines.

Indus scribes used the same cognitive shortcut.

- one stroke ‚Üí simplest category

Indus strokes show a **controlled rhythm**,

## **11.9 Did Indus Strokes Always Mean Numbers?  
A Pluralistic View**

We cannot assume:

- ‚Äúthree strokes = the number 3‚Äù

Pluralism means **we allow all possibilities**

For example:

- A jar sign + 3 strokes might mean  
  ‚Äúthird kind of jar,‚Äù  
  not ‚Äúthree jars.‚Äù

  ‚Äúcategory B of the fish family,‚Äù

This protects against oversimplification.

## **11.10 Patterns That Suggest Number-Like Behaviour**

Even without knowing ‚Äúmeaning,‚Äù  
we can detect **mathematical behaviour**:

### **1. Bounded Variants**
Most core signs have 1, 2, 3, or 4-stroke versions ‚Äî never 10 or 12.

### **2. Ordered Sequences**
Strokes typically increase from left ‚Üí right  
or from top ‚Üí bottom.

### **3. Controlled Repetition**
A sign rarely repeats strokes randomly.

### **4. Positional Sensitivity**
Changing the position of strokes  
(prefix vs suffix)  
changes the cluster.

### **5. Rare High Counts**
4-stroke signs are rarer, suggesting special significance  
(as in many early counting traditions).

These are clues for students to observe without forcing meaning.

## **11.7 Sumerian Counting: Wedges, Tallies & Clay Accounting**

Before Sumerian cuneiform became a full script,  
it existed as:

- **tokens**,  
- **tallies**,  
- **wedge-based numbers**,  
- **bundles representing goods**.

Sumerians used:

- vertical wedges for 1  
- repeated impressions for quantity  
- circles and cones to mark types  

These combined into a proto-numerical accounting system  
that gradually became writing.

Across Sumerian, Tamil, and Indus contexts we see:

### **Shared Features**
- strokes ‚Üí counts  
- repetition ‚Üí quantity  
- position ‚Üí category  
- shape ‚Üí classification  
- short sequences ‚Üí compact meaning  

These similarities arise from **human needs**,  
not necessarily from cultural contact.

## **11.8 Why Early Mathematics Is Always Visual**

Long before arithmetic became symbolic,  
mathematics was:

- **seen** as marks,  
- **stacked** as tallies,  
- **grouped** as bundles,  
- **arranged** as lines and dots,  
- **felt** as clay impressions.

This visual mathematics:

- trains the eye,  
- builds proportional thinking,  
- teaches grouping,  
- strengthens structural memory.

Indus seals display this visual mathematical intelligence:  
3‚Äì4 strokes are never random;  
they behave like carefully placed qualifiers.

Tamil and Sumerian traditions echo the same cognitive pattern:  
structured marks ‚Üí structured thought.

## **11.11 Toward a Pluralistic Number Logic**

We do not know:

- if the Indus script encoded numbers directly,  
- if strokes always meant quantity,  
- if signs mapped to spoken numerals,  
- if the system was phonetic or non-phonetic.

But we **do** know:

- strokes behave consistently,  
- their placement is meaningful,  
- families of signs modify each other,  
- short inscriptions compress information,  
- patterns mirror early Sumerian and Tamil traditions.

Thus, students can adopt a **pluralistic number logic**:

### **Possibility A ‚Äî Strokes = Numbers**  
(1, 2, 3, 4)

### **Possibility B ‚Äî Strokes = Qualifiers**  
(type, class, grade, level)

### **Possibility C ‚Äî Strokes = Ritual cycles**  
(steps, offerings, phases)

### **Possibility D ‚Äî Strokes = Administrative markers**  
(batch, category, inspection count)

Each model can be tested against inscriptions  
without insisting on a single answer.
